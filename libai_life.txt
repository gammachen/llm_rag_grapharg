Here's the English translation of the detailed biography of Li Bai (Li Po), covering his life from birth to death, including travels, relationships, and literary achievements.

---

**701 AD** – Li Bai was born in Suyab, a city in the Western Regions (present-day Tokmok, Kyrgyzstan). His father, Li Ke, was a merchant, and the family was well-off. At the age of five, Li Bai moved with his family back to the interior of China, settling in Changlong County, Mianzhou, Jiannan Circuit (present-day Jiangyou, Sichuan). He grew up in Qinglian Township, where he studied and practiced swordsmanship. To this day, a site called “Li Bai’s Reading Platform” remains in his hometown.

**Around 715 AD** – At fourteen, Li Bai began studying at Kuang Mountain (within present-day Jiangyou, Sichuan), where he stayed for ten years. In the quiet solitude of the mountains, he read classics, practiced swordplay, gathered herbs, and played the qin. Occasionally he went down to the market, drinking and chatting with local hunters and woodcutters. These mountain years shaped his chivalrous, independent character and his disdain for power and wealth.

**Autumn 720 AD** – At nineteen, Li Bai left the mountains for the first time and traveled to Zizhou (present-day Santai, Sichuan) to visit the renowned scholar Zhao Rui. Zhao lived in seclusion on Changping Mountain and was famous for his knowledge of political strategy. Li Bai stayed with him for a month, studying statecraft and the *Changjing Ce* (A Classic of Persuasion). Upon parting, Zhao Rui told him, “The empire will soon fall into chaos; you should seek office through your literary talent.”

**Spring 724 AD** – At twenty-three, Li Bai bid farewell to his parents and began a decade-long journey of wandering. “Leaving home with a sword, saying goodbye to kin for distant travels,” he followed the Min River downstream and left Sichuan via Yuzhou (present-day Chongqing). In Jiangling (present-day Jingzhou, Hubei), he met the Taoist master Sima Chengzhen. Sima looked at Li Bai and said, “You possess an immortal’s air and bone structure; you may roam with me to the ends of the heavens.” Delighted, Li Bai wrote “The Great Roc Meets the Rare Bird.”

**Autumn 725 AD** – Li Bai arrived at Jinling (present-day Nanjing). He rented a small cottage by the Qinhuai River and stayed for three months. By day, he visited the ruins of the Six Dynasties; by night, he recited poetry and competed in drinking games at taverns. Soon his funds ran out, and he had to pawn his sword. Before winter arrived, he went downstream to Yangzhou. Yangzhou was then the most prosperous commercial center, and Li Bai spent money like water, “scattering more than three hundred thousand taels in less than a year,” supporting many struggling scholars.

**Winter 726 AD** – Li Bai fell gravely ill in Yangzhou, lying alone in an inn with no one to care for him. After recovering, he wrote “Quiet Night Thought”: “Before my bed the moonlight shines / Like frost upon the ground. / I raise my head to gaze at the moon, / Lower it, and think of home.” After the New Year, he left Yangzhou and eventually made his way to Anlu (present-day Anlu, Hubei).

**727 AD** – At twenty-seven, in Anlu, Li Bai met the retired chancellor Xu Yushi. Xu admired Li Bai’s talent and married his granddaughter, Lady Xu, to him. Li Bai settled in Anlu, living at Peach Blossom Cliff at the foot of Baizhao Mountain. After marriage, he tilled the fields, grew vegetables, read, and wrote poetry, living several years of a relatively stable farming life.

**Spring 730 AD** – Li Bai decided to seek office once more and went to Chang’an in pursuit of a career. He first visited Princess Yuzhen at Zhongnan Mountain and then called upon various powerful court figures, but none recommended him. After more than a year in Chang’an, his money was gone and he could only stay in a small inn south of the city. In winter, with heavy snow, he suffered from cold and hunger. Later, recalling those days, he wrote, “I strummed my sword and sang bitter songs; trailing my robe at princely gates, I found no favor.”

**Autumn 732 AD** – Li Bai left Chang’an and wandered to Mount Song. He searched for the Taoist alchemist Jiao Lianshi but failed to meet him. He then went to Luoyang and stayed for six months, exchanging verses with local literati. By then, Li Bai’s reputation as a poet was growing, but he still held no office.

**735 AD** – Li Bai moved his entire family from Anlu to the eastern region of Shandong (Luzhou), settling in Rencheng (present-day Jining, Shandong). He bought a few acres of land by the Wenshui River and had local farmers tend them, while he himself traveled frequently between Rencheng, Yanzhou, and Mount Tai. Around this time, he met Kong Chaofu, Han Zhun, and three other recluses; together they formed a drinking society in the bamboo groves of Culai Mountain, known as the “Six Idlers of Bamboo Creek.”

**739 AD** – Li Bai’s wife, Lady Xu, died of illness. Deeply grieved, he took his son and daughter and moved to a tavern outside the east gate of Yanzhou. By day, he drank and discussed poetry with patrons; by night, he cared for the children. The following year, he met a widow named Liu and thought of remarrying, but Liu looked down on him for being poor and without rank, often speaking harshly. Angered, Li Bai left her.

**Autumn 742 AD** – At forty-one, Li Bai finally saw a turning point. Emperor Xuanzong issued an imperial summons calling him to the capital. Overjoyed, he wrote: “Laughing wildly, I head out the door – how could I be one to dwell among the common grass?” In Chang’an, the emperor received him with great ceremony, “stepping down from his carriage to greet him as if meeting a divine immortal,” and appointed him a Hanlin Academician (Hanlin gongfeng) – an unofficial role responsible for composing poetry and prose for the emperor’s entertainment.

**743 AD** – Li Bai lived in the Hanlin Academy, daily writing poems for court occasions. In spring, he accompanied the emperor to view peonies at Chenxiang Pavilion in Xingqing Palace and was ordered to write three “Pure Peace Tunes.” The line “Her robe is a cloud, her face a flower” became famous. But soon Li Bai grew tired of such ornate versifying. He often drank himself drunk in taverns, carousing with He Zhizhang, Zhang Xu, and other members of the “Eight Immortals of the Wine Cup.” Once, drunk and summoned to compose a poem, he had the powerful eunuch Gao Lishi take off his boots, thereby offending the palace elite.

**Spring 744 AD** – Emperor Xuanzong “honorably dismissed” Li Bai with a gift of gold – a polite way of expelling him from Chang’an. After leaving, he first stayed in Luoyang for a few days, where he met Du Fu, eleven years his junior. They became close friends and traveled together to Liang and Song (present-day Shangqiu, Henan). In autumn, Gao Shi joined them. The three poets climbed the Shandou Terrace, rode horses, hunted, drank, and composed verses in what became the legendary “Liang-Song excursion.”

**745 AD** – Li Bai and Du Fu met again in eastern Shandong. Together they toured Yanzhou and visited the hermit Fan Shi. Du Fu wrote the line: “Drunk, we slept in autumn with the same quilt; hand in hand, we roamed by day.” In winter, they parted at Shimen in Lu Commandery and never saw each other again. Thereafter, Li Bai traveled south alone to Jinling, Yangzhou, and elsewhere, living on the generosity of friends and officials.

**748 AD** – Li Bai came to Mount Lu and built a thatched cottage at Screen Cliff, next to Five Old Men Peak, intending to retire there. He had servants and tenant farmers on the mountain and kept a pair of silver pheasants. The following year, his daughter Pingyang married, and his son Boqin stayed with him. But peace did not last long – war soon arrived.

**Winter 755 AD** – The An Lushan Rebellion broke out. Li Bai fled with his family, first to Dangtu (present-day Dangtu, Anhui), then to Xuancheng (present-day Xuancheng, Anhui), and finally into the deep mountains of Shanxian (present-day Shengzhou, Zhejiang). Along the way, witnessing the suffering of the people, he wrote: “Piled white bones rise like hills – how have the common people offended Heaven?”

**Winter 756 AD** – Prince Yong, Li Lin, sailed his fleet east and, passing by Jiujiang, sent three missions up the mountain to recruit Li Bai. Believing this was a chance to serve his country, Li Bai gladly accepted and became an advisor in Prince Yong’s staff. He accompanied the prince by boat from Jiangling to Yangzhou, composing eleven “Songs of Prince Yong’s Eastern Campaign.” Only later did he realize that the emperor (Suzong) had already branded Prince Yong a rebel.

**757 AD** – Prince Yong’s forces were defeated. Li Bai fled back to Pengze County and surrendered himself. He was sentenced to prison. From his cell, he wrote letters begging for help from old friends. His wife, Lady Zong, ran about and finally secured his release. But in autumn of the same year, the court changed his sentence to perpetual exile in Yelang (present-day Tongzi, Guizhou). Li Bai set out on the road to exile, passing through Jiangling, Yiling, and Kuizhou, then entering the Three Gorges.

**Winter 758 AD** – On the exile route, Li Bai wrote “Up the Three Gorges”: “Three days passing Yellow Ox, / Three nights moving too slow. / Three days and three nights again, / Unaware my temples grow white with snow.” By then he was fifty-seven, his health failing. Cold and hungry along the way, he survived on the kindness of local officials and commoners.

**Spring 759 AD** – When Li Bai reached Baidi Town, news arrived of a general amnesty. Overjoyed, he immediately turned his boat downstream, writing the immortal “Leaving Baidi in the Morning”: “Leaving Baidi at dawn, framed in rosy clouds, / A thousand miles to Jiangling, covered in one day. / The gibbons’ cries from either bank never end, / My light skiff has passed ten thousand peaks.” Afterwards, he wandered through Jiangxia, Yueyang, Changsha, and other places, living on friends’ support.

**760 AD** – Li Bai arrived in Xuancheng and revisited Mount Jingting, writing the lines: “All birds fly high away, / A single cloud drifts alone in leisure. / Never tiring of gazing at each other, / Only Jingting Mountain.” In autumn, he went again to Jinling, staying at the home of his old friend Quan Zhaoyi. By now his health was very poor, and he was often bedridden.

**761 AD** – At sixty-one, Li Bai reached Dangtu and sought refuge with his clan uncle Li Yangbing, the county magistrate. Li Yangbing housed him in a small room next to the yamen and sent for physicians and medicine. Sensing his end was near, Li Bai sorted his life’s poetry into ten volumes and handed them to Li Yangbing, asking him to write a preface.

**Winter 762 AD** – Li Bai died of illness in Dangtu at the age of sixty-two. On his deathbed, he wrote “On the Point of Death”: “The great roc flies, shaking the eight directions, / But halfway up, its strength fails.” Legend has it that he died drunk, jumping into the Yangtze to embrace the moon – though unhistorical, it became the ending people most wished to believe. He was buried at Dragon Mountain in Dangtu, later moved to the foot of Green Mountain, where his grave remains today.
